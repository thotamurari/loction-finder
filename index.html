UK-Based Andhra Couple Accused of 5 Crore Visa Scam; Over 100 Victims Reportedly Affected

Punjab / Amritsar / Updated: May 28, 2025, 12:00 IST
Venkat and Indhu Bathula allegedly involved in the visa scam

In a disturbing case of large-scale immigration fraud, an Indian couple originally from Kondamanjuluru, Prakasam District, Andhra Pradesh, has been accused of duping over 100 Indian nationals out of nearly 5 crore under the pretense of offering UK work sponsorships.

The couple identified as Venkat (Venkateswarlu) Bathula and his wife Indhu Bathula were reportedly residing in the United Kingdom when they launched what many victims now describe as a calculated and emotionally manipulative scam.

According to multiple accounts from victims, the Bathulas gained trust by initiating friendships, projecting warmth and credibility, and gradually introducing their so-called "consultancy services." The couple claimed they could help Indian nationals legally settle and work in the UK under various job categories including caregiving, IT, hospitality, and pharmacy.

"They were very polite and convincing at first. They said they wanted to help people from small towns get a better life," one victim shared on condition of anonymity. "But once we paid them, communication dropped. We began to realise they were operating fraudulently."

Reports suggest the Bathulas issued fake sponsorship letters and documentation to hopeful migrants, charging amounts ranging from 3 lakh to 6 lakh per individual. The total value of payments is estimated to exceed 5 crore.

As suspicion grew and more victims began comparing their experiences, the couple allegedly attempted to cover up the fraud by fabricating a personal tragedy. Venkat Bathula is said to have staged a suicide attempt, complete with photos and videos of him in a hospital, purportedly to evoke sympathy and deter legal or public action.

Shortly after this incident, both Venkat and Indhu Bathula reportedly returned to India. Their current whereabouts remain unclear, although some sources believe they have returned to their native Andhra village or may be hiding elsewhere in India.

This scam has left many victims devastated financially, emotionally, and legally. Some individuals had resigned from their jobs, taken out loans, or even sold property to fund their dreams of working abroad. Now, many are grappling with debt and hopelessness.

"This is not just fraud, it's emotional and psychological exploitation," said a UK-based legal expert familiar with immigration abuse cases. "The number of victims and the volume of money involved makes this a serious case of transnational crime."
